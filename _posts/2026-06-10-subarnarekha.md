
A refugee family resettles near an industrial landscape by a river, hoping to rebuild their lives after Partition. Years of struggle, separation and social prejudice lead to the collapse of those hopes. Epic in feeling, yet intimate in the pain it conveys, Ghatak’s film fuses melodrama, myth and politics into a tragic vision of a new nation scarred by the Partition.  
**bfi.org.uk**

4 November 2025 marked Ritwik Ghatak’s 100th birthday. That is, it would have if he hadn’t passed away back in 1976 at the age of just 50 (6 February was the 50th anniversary of his death). The brilliant Bengali director is a peripheral figure for most western cinephiles, though they may well be aware of his sublime _The Cloud-Capped Star_ (1960), thanks to its inclusion in the Criterion Collection, and possibly _A River Called Titas_ (1973), restored by Martin Scorsese’s World Cinema Project. In India itself, Ghatak is celebrated as one of the trio of great 20th-century filmmakers, alongside Satyajit Ray and Mrinal Sen, and _Subarnarekha_ (filmed in 1962 but released three years later) is recognised as a classic.

I encountered _Subarnarekha_ in the bowels of the BFI’s Stephen Street office in London, a few floors below the _Sight and Sound_ office. This was in 1997, and I had been invited to write an article for _Time Out_ about a Ghatak season at the National Film Theatre (as it then was), timed to mark the 50th anniversary of India’s independence and concomitant partition from Pakistan, a pivotal episode in Ghatak’s life and work. I really didn’t know anything about Ghatak going into the screening room, but when I came out 132 minutes later I was shaken and inspired. Indeed, I’ve been banging the drum for this movie ever since (and twice voted for it in this magazine’s Greatest Films poll).

_Subarnarekha_ is the name of a river in West Bengal, known for its trace deposits of gold. This river winds its metaphorical way through the film, a symbol of hope, and/or false hope (fool’s gold). It is the story of Ishwar, a Hindu teacher displaced from the north by Partition and caught up in a refugee colony in 1948 with his much younger sister, Sita. In the film’s opening scenes, he adopts an orphaned lower caste boy of Sita’s age, Abhi, when the child’s mother is snatched off the street by armed thugs. Later, a chance encounter with an old college friend yields a job offer in a provincial factory a position that will allow Ishwar to escape the camp and secure a future for Sita and Abhi, even if it means putting his family above the needs of his community. ‘The British have a word for people like you,’ his idealistic fellow teacher Haraprasad reprimands him: ‘Deserter.’

A few years later, Ishwar’s choice seems to have paid off. He’s to be promoted to manager. Sita has grown into a lovely young woman. Abhi is about to graduate and Ishwar plans to send him to Germany to study engineering. But the younger man aspires to become a novelist, and worse, he and Sita are in love. When his employer becomes aware of Abhi’s caste, Ishwar is persuaded to send him back to Calcutta to make his own way in the world, and to arrange for Sita to be married off. It’s a complete betrayal of principles that sets up the film’s tragic third act.

Ghatak described his conception of cinema as a weapon to raise public consciousness, and this is a more overtly political film than _The Cloud-Capped Star_. The first words we hear are ‘Long live the Motherland’ as the Indian flag is raised over a new school in the refugee colony, but it’s clear that Ghatak sympathises with Haraprasad’s sense that the country was betrayed from its inception. With news of the assassination of Mahatma Gandhi, gunshots break over the diegetic soundtrack, the first salvo in the director’s Brechtian experiments with sound.

An iconoclast, Ghatak infuses a predominantly naturalistic but heavily freighted _mise en scène_ with striking compositions, fluid camera movement and cutting that seems designed to keep us asking questions. Despite the poverty of its settings (the arid banks of the shallow river; an abandoned British air force base; a slum apartment), the movie often feels very modern in its uninhibited application of lessons from Welles, Eisenstein and the _nouvelle vague_. Equally, Ghatak’s commitment to idealists and underdogs – exiles, refugees, orphans, women, artists, activists – still rings loud and clear 60 years later.

Less modern, perhaps, is the filmmaker’s sternly Marxist moralism. Ghatak had a clear sense of where the world was heading: ‘Calcutta is full of fun now,’ Haraprasad tells Ishwar towards the end, when both men have lost all hope. ‘Such horrible fun! You will be stunned to see how people let themselves go with the current. The way of the herd is Truth. Indulgence is the way to liberation. Letting oneself be swept away with the current. Such intoxication! You lose everything and still want more.’

It’s perhaps not too much to hope for that this incendiary and inspiring piece of filmmaking should merit wider recognition in the west. A film of this power and poetry must surely prevail. At least this is an illusion we can cling to, like that utopian golden river, glinting in the distance.  
**Tom Charity, _Sight and Sound_, March 2026**
